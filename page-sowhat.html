Sushi rooted in
everyday life and seasons

Blessed by mountain, field, river, and sea ingredients, Toyama’s people have cultivated a unique culinary culture long before the region was named Toyama. Sushi made with salmon, including cherry salmon, is documented in texts from the Heian period (794–1185).
For more than 600 years, temple feasts at Zuisenji and Zentokuji included mackerel sushi as part of daytime meals.
Fermented river fish sushi, the precursor to today’s masu-zushi, was created during the Edo period
(1603-1868) by a samurai of the Toyama domain.
Buri-zushi, which sandwiches yellowtail with turnip and ferments it with koji, originated between the mid-Edo and Taisho periods (1912-1926).

「新しい寿司文化を生む人々」 の
取材・紹介

After World War II, the nigiri sushi culture, born and developed in Edo (Tokyo), spread to Toyama. Local chefs have leveraged Toyama’s rich ingredients to establish a distinct nigiri sushi culture. Today,
Michelin-starred sushi restaurants have emerged, and nationally renowned chefs and chains have opened branches in Toyama.
Moreover, young chefs continue to innovate, creating sushi that challenges traditional norms. Toyama Prefecture and Toyama City even feature “sushi” as a key promotional keyword, and the region’s sushi diversity continues to expand.

About Us

Mission & Vision

The project was born from the Toyama Growth Strategy Council and is led by Shiawase Design, a public-private partnership organization tackling social challenges through town development. It aims to create new tourism markets by leveraging local cultural assets and nurturing the next generation of talent.
